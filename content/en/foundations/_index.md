Hand a task to AI and the answer shows up quickly. Whether you can use that answer as is, though, is a separate question. The power to tell where it is right and where it slipped comes, in the end, from the fundamentals.

The eight tracks gathered here are those fundamentals. Click a tile on the map below and you go straight into that field. For now the big skeleton is in place, and the concepts underneath are being filled in one at a time.

## Why these eight

They were chosen so that each asks something different, and so that together they leave no large gap. Passing once through form, mind, nature, and society is what lets you judge an AI's answer in the real world.

- **Form & Computation** (mathematics, computer science): what is true, and what can be computed.
- **Mind & Meaning** (cognitive science, philosophy): how people come to know, and what is right and meaningful.
- **Nature & Making** (physics, engineering): what rules reality follows, and what can be built.
- **Society & Institutions** (economics, law): how limited resources are divided, and what is allowed.

The fields borrow content from one another. Computer science borrows from mathematics, and engineering borrows from physics. Even so, the questions they ask differ, and no one of them can stand in for another. AI often starts where computer science and cognitive science meet, but to put it to use in the real world you need a feel for the other fields too.
