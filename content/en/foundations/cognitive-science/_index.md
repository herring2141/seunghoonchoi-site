AI was built in the image of human intelligence, and in the end it is used on people. So once you know how people see, remember, and judge, and where they keep going wrong, you can see how AI can help a person and how it can nudge them.

The most useful thing cognitive science offers is this: human mistakes are not random; they lean in regular directions. Once you know the lean, you start looking again at both your own judgments and the screens other people built for you.

## Why it matters more in the AI era

The places where AI helps a person and the places where it sways one both sit on these gaps in cognition. This is what explains why a recommendation feed is so hard to put down, and why a confident-but-wrong answer from AI is so easy to believe. Knowing the weak spots in your own judgment lets you design AI better and use it without being pushed around.

## Where it leads

Cognitive science takes the question of knowing from [philosophy](../philosophy/) and moves it into experiments, and the AI of [computer science](../computer-science/) borrowed many of its ideas from these models of cognition. Knowing where people deceive themselves also helps in [economics](../economics/), where the goal is to understand the choices people make.
