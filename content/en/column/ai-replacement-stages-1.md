What you're good at is about to become common. And it goes first. There's a pattern in the order AI takes over human work, and the ones standing at the very front of the line are, of all things, the jobs with a set answer.

Point your phone's camera at a menu in a foreign restaurant and it turns into your own language on the spot. Ten years ago you'd have opened a dictionary and fumbled through it one word at a time. At first it feels amazing, and before long you use it without a second thought. This little scene holds the first button in that order. It starts with the work whose answer anyone can see, the work where you can check right away whether it's right or wrong.

This piece walks through those first five stages. The first three are work that gets verified as "this is correct." The last two are the seat where the work gets carried all the way through, and the seat where a person watches over it.

## AI is strongest where the work gets scored

First, one thing to flag. The work AI is unusually strong at has something in common. It's work that gets scored right or wrong on the spot.

In Go you win or you lose. How a protein folds, you find out by matching it against the real structure. Code with tests attached either passes or throws up a red line. In this kind of work, AI tries again and again, fixes its mistakes immediately, and tries again. A person has to sleep, and one slip can drain the will to keep going. A machine, as long as the scoring is clear, runs toward that score all night long. So the clearer the answer, the faster the job goes. The next three stages all belong here.

**Stage 1: work with a fixed format.** Simple translation, basic coding, fill-in-the-form reports, calculation, summarizing long texts. Work with a clear answer that used to take a lot of effort by hand. The menu translation above is one example, and so is the autocomplete that fills in the next line the moment a programmer types one. In this kind of work, "I do this fast and accurately" was the skill itself, and that skill is the first to become common.

**Stage 2: the expert's analysis.** This is where people start to be surprised. A doctor reading an X-ray or a CT scan to pin down a disease. The work of forecasting demand and drawing up a design. Judgments only people who studied for years could make. But diagnosis and prediction, when you get right down to it, are "looking at the material in front of you and picking the right answer within a set framework." A set framework means it can be scored, and if it can be scored, it becomes work AI is strong at. The expert doesn't disappear. They just give up the seat of "the person who does this best."

**Stage 3: reading people's minds.** This is the most unexpected part. People tend to believe that "AI may not know the data, but only a human knows the human heart." Yet recommendation algorithms guess fairly well what we'll tap next. Ads find the spot where we hesitate, and they're getting better and better at predicting which content will take off. It's no surprise. AI learned, from the traces billions of people left behind, what pulls them in and what bores them. The people one person meets in a lifetime number a few thousand. AI sees a record of the human heart that is beyond comparison. Reading people's minds, far from being the last line of defense, is closer to the kind of work AI does best, precisely because the scoring is clear.

Tie the three stages into one line and it comes out like this. If producing the answer is what makes me valuable, that value is the first to get pushed out.

## The seat that runs the work all the way also shakes

Up to here it's been the story of AI producing answers. The next two stages are different. We look at what happens not just to producing answers, but to the seat that runs the work itself, and the seat where a person watches over it.

**Stage 4: many steps, all the way through, on its own.** The old AI was an assistant you asked once and got one answer. Now, give it a single goal and it walks through the middle steps on its own. It finds the material, organizes it, fills in the forms, processes it, and moves on to the next step. Picture the flow inside a company where a document automatically rides up through the sign-off stages. The middle work people used to handle by hand shrinks, and the person narrows down to just deciding "what to do." In effect they get pushed up, from the seat that does the work to the seat that sets the direction.

**Stage 5: the moment oversight gets in the way instead.** This is the most important turn in this piece. We usually believe that having a person check one more time makes things safer. Often that's true. But when two things are met together, the story flips. When AI gets it wrong less often than a person, and on top of that, when the work can be undone even if it goes wrong.

At that point a person's review stops being a safeguard and becomes noise. When a person pores over a result that's almost all correct, they fix perfectly good parts for no reason, slow the work down, or slip in new mistakes that weren't there. Picture a reviewer who, trying to catch typos in text that was typed flawlessly, touches a perfectly good sentence and only makes things slower. So the seat that "checks one more time" quietly disappears. The only thing that doesn't disappear is responsibility. Without ever actually looking, one person's name stays on, in name only, as the one who takes the blame if something goes wrong.

There's one caveat here. Stage 5 is, after all, a story limited to work that competes over "who's more accurate." In a seat that competes on accuracy, human review becomes noise. But oversight that decides not accuracy but "who takes responsibility" and "what matters more" is a different grain. That isn't something you can win on accuracy, so it lasts far longer. We'll handle that story separately in a later stage.

## So, on to the next part

This is the front end of the landscape of desk work, the work done with the head. The clearer the answer, the faster it goes, and at the point where human review becomes noise, the seat quietly empties out.

One natural objection remains. "Then isn't work done with the hands, the feel learned through the body, safe?" Part 2 follows that question. From repetitive factory work to manual skill, and on to the "gut feel" that looks like it'll hold out a long time, Stages 6 to 8 show why the physical world goes more slowly, but, in the end, goes all the same.
