You're dropped into the middle of an unfamiliar city. No map, no signal on your phone. What's the fastest way to grasp this place? The answer is not "walk a lot." Wander down a hundred alleys at random and your legs ache while your head stays a tangle.

Fast people check exactly three directions. Once straight ahead, once forty-five degrees to the left, once forty-five degrees to the right. Where do the main roads run? Which side are the tall buildings clustered on? Where's a fixed landmark, a river or a hill? Those three directions alone give you a minimum set of coordinates: downtown is that way, the edge of town is this way. From then on, every alley you walk drops neatly onto the map already in your head.

A new field works the same way. People often ask, "How many cases do I need to see before I get a feel for this?", as if there were one magic number. But dropping in and out of various fields taught me something. The answer isn't a single number. It's a range that differs from layer to layer. And there's a deeper truth underneath: what matters in the end is not the count of cases but the angle and the feedback. The same idea as walking the same alley a hundred times being worse than seeing three different directions once each.

## Three cases to set your direction

The first thing you need is exactly three viewpoints. Like the three directions in the city.

First, what does good output look like? If you're learning to cook, you have to see, at least once, the cross-section of a well-seared steak, the feel of a well-composed photo. Without a target image, you can't even tell whether what you made turned out well or fell apart.

Second, what decides the result? When the same ingredients lead one person to success and another to failure, what's the variable behind the difference? For a steak: heat level, cooking time, how long you let the meat rest. In any field, the core variables that really steer the outcome are surprisingly few.

Third, where do beginners fall apart? Where does a newcomer always slip? Not heating the pan enough, flipping the meat over and over. Knowing other people's failure points ahead of time lets you skip half your own trial and error.

These three viewpoints set your direction. It's not about seeing more. It's seeing once each from three different angles. That's the start.

## The whiteboard in your head has only seven slots

Once you have a direction, it's time to build a small instrument panel in your head. Just as a car's dashboard puts the speedometer, fuel gauge, and engine temperature in one glance, every new field has its own set of key gauges to watch at once.

But these gauges get into trouble past seven or so. The familiar "seven plus or minus two." The idea that the number of chunks a person's working memory can hold at one time is roughly five to nine. The whiteboard in your head is smaller than you think. If you can't boil the core down to seven or eight and try to hold twenty at once, the board fills up and there's no room for new information to land.

Take writing. As a beginner you try to mind spelling, word choice, sentence length, paragraph structure, logical flow, the reader, and the title all at once, and your mind stalls. The skilled writer, by contrast, bundles "spelling and word choice" into one chunk called "line editing." Several things compress into one, and a hand comes free. With that free hand they grip something bigger. The ability to keep the panel inside seven slots: that's the doorway to intermediate.

## Gather cases by kind, not by count

Once you have a panel, it's time to gather cases in earnest. Here's a common trap. People obsess over "a lot," when what really matters is "different kinds."

The point where repeating patterns start to become visible is roughly twenty to thirty cases. But a decisive condition is attached. Those cases have to contrast with each other. The success, the failure, the ambiguous in-between, the extreme, and the most common standard case. Only when you lay out cases of such different colors does a rule finally show its outline: "ah, this variable works like that, here."

By contrast, thirty cases of the same kind are really just one case seen thirty times. Someone who has only looked at thirty photos of perfectly seared steaks has no idea what failure looks like. Memorize thirty easy sentences of the same level in a foreign language and you never meet the moment a sentence collapses or the register shifts. Repetition without contrast only adds volume; it doesn't draw the outline. When you gather cases, don't count the number. Count the kinds.

## If you don't fix it, the bad form sets

Seeing with your eyes and doing with your hands are different problems. The pattern becoming visible doesn't mean your hand will follow.

That instability peculiar to beginners. It worked yesterday but not today, you did the same thing yet got a different result. That wobble dies down somewhere around seventy to two hundred repetitions. It's like learning an instrument: you have to play the same passage about a hundred times before your hand moves on its own. A workout form settling into the body, the nerves draining out of public speaking, they land somewhere in here too.

But one more thing has to be said here. These repetitions can't just be a "count"; they have to be a count with feedback attached. Repeat a hundred times without knowing you're wrong and you'll just set the wrong form a hundred times over. Do it once, check where it went off, fix it, and do it again. Only with this corrective loop do the seventy reps mean anything.

## The outline shows when you bundle

Behind these numbers, 3, 7, 30, 100, one engine is actually running. Chunking. The ability to bundle scattered information into one meaningful unit.

The outline doesn't appear when information grows. It appears when information clumps into chunks. A chess master remembers the board at a glance not because of raw memory but because they see twenty pieces as a few chunks: "that's a common attacking formation, that's a defensive shape." Twenty scattered dots to a beginner are three or four meaningful clumps to the master.

So the moment a new field's outline clicks into place is not the moment you cram in more information. It's the moment scattered things suddenly bind into one chunk. "Oh, these were all the same thing." In that instant a slot on your mental panel empties, and with the empty slot you grip something bigger. People who learn fast aren't the ones who memorize a lot. They're the ones who bundle often.

## Correction beats reading

Finally, one principle that runs through every number. Feedback beats exposure.

Trying it yourself thirty times with a correction each time beats reading something passively a hundred times. Between someone who only listened to a foreign language for a hundred hours and someone who stumbled through speaking it thirty times, getting "no, you say it like this" every time, anyone who's done it knows who improves faster. Reading and listening are comfortable. So we keep fleeing that way. But what carves the outline is the uncomfortable process of cutting into it yourself and checking where it came out wrong.

Exposure piles up the material; feedback carves that material into a shape. Pile the material mountain-high and never carve it once, and you can stare at it for ten years without the outline ever appearing.

## The order is 3, 7, 30, 100

Tie all of this into one path and it comes out like this.

First, set your direction with **three angles**. What good output looks like, what decides the result, where beginners fall apart. Next, distill the core into **seven chunks** to build the panel in your head. Then gather **thirty contrasting cases**. By kind, not by number. Success, failure, ambiguous, extreme, standard. Finally, run **a hundred feedback loops**. Not mere repetition, but a do, check, fix loop.

Three, then seven, then thirty, then a hundred. These numbers are not laws. They're just the working breakpoints, the places where, in a practitioner's experience, something tends to shift. Depending on the field they might double or halve. What truly matters isn't the numbers themselves but the two principles underneath them. The outline comes from angle, not volume; skill comes from feedback, not exposure. That story about not walking a hundred alleys in a strange city but checking three directions was, all along, a map that applies just the same to every new thing you set out to learn.
